Elijah Beaton is a  Historian,  Ph.D. Student, and Designer.

My design skills include Figma, Illustrator, and Photoshop, as well as HTML and CSS. I have also dabbled in some Swift UI.

I am a strong believer in designing with an ethos of negative space. Rather than try to exert control over every pixel in every possible edge case, I believe in design as a series of flexible guidelines. This requires approaching a design problem with a holistic view, but it offloads the calculations to the browser or the system. Embracing this flexibility enables a prioritization of the most important user-oriented details and interactions.

Paul Lucas Graduate Conference

Print and Social Media Campaign, 2022

I believe that good design is that sense of harmony between the aesthetic and the goals of a project.

After a 3-year hiatus, the relaunch of the Paul Lucas Graduate Conference needed a design that would stand out visually from other grad conferences. This design was meant to be both cheap and efficient to print posters, as well as increase legibility in a social media feed.

Personal Academic Website, 2021

The aesthetic of a project should serve a purpose beyond looking nice. It should offer cues as to function, uses, and possibilities. It bridges the gap between the project and the user.

I wanted this design to offer both an overview of my work in a way that was flexible enough to encompass both my academic and non-academic interests.

Personal Academic Website, 2021

The design serves the goal rather than the opposite. The goal for this site was simply to publicize a Curriculum Vitae.

The design is an exploration of defaults. Drawing on both unstyled HTML and the standard academic paper design, the goal was to emphasize the text. The design itself was meant to polish and modernize those defaults styles, creating a fast, simple, and accessible site.

Kitchens in Better Homes and Gardens

Short Run Color Zine, 2020

I was experimenting with alternate ways to present my research into art, architecture, and design, and this zine reignited a semi-dormant interest in design. Part of this experiment was scaling the gap between an idea in Figma, Illustrator, or Photoshop and a physical result. Actually printing and distributing the zine was an important step in designing for a larger audience than myself.
